Possession

Possession is marked in two different ways depending on if it is alienable or inalienable.

Inalienable Inalienable possession is marked by compounding the words with the connector སཉི [sqa].

Example: "The man's child": ཡྷོབིུསཉིཔེཔེ (DEF+man+POSS+child)

Alienable Alienable possession is marked by the particle སྙི [səqa], placed before the object that is being possessed. If the subject of the clause is the possessed object, then possession is marked by putting the particle before the verb instead.

Examples: I see the man's cat: ཡྷོབིུ་སྙི་ཀོཁིཡ

Descriptives

After the main subject-object body of a word come a series of suffixes called "descriptives". Although roots are very often either primarily descriptive roots or primarily noun roots, which one they are in a given word is determined by their placement rather than their sound.

The category of descriptive is known by the descriptive marker that precedes it. The marker does not need to immediately precede it: instead, the marker applies to all following descriptives until the next marker is used.

There are three descriptive markers: 1. The adjectival descriptive marker རི [ha]. This marker tell you that the descriptives that follow it modify the object to which the marker is attached. 2. The adverbal descriptive marker རོ [hu]. This marker tell you that the descriptives that follow it modify the verb of the clause of which the attached object is an object. Depending on the descriptor, it also can make it modify the event described by the clause in general. It is typically applied only to objects that directly precede the verb, or to the direct object of the verb, though it can in principle be marked on any object. 3. The subject adjectival descriptive marker རིུ [hĩ]. This marker tells you that the descriptives that follow it modify the subject of the clause of which the attached object is an object. It is, almost as a rule, applied only to objects with attached subjects.
